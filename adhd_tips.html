Tips for Teachers

1. Tips and Tricks

These strategies are not necessarily required for a certain diagnosis but can help make learning easier for children with ADHD.

Math

  • Give students more time for exams so they don't feel pressured. Try to steer clear of timed facts tests.
  • Regularly verify students' work for accuracy. Set a limit on the number of problems they must complete before allowing them to proceed (e.g., one row or four or five problems), and ensure they are verified.
  • Provide a list of steps or approaches to techniques and multi-step tasks. Provide students with a desk copy model of the processes required to solve problems or post steps with clear numbers.

Writing

  • Allow the use of speech-to-text programs, such as Dragon Dictation or the free feature found in Google Docs.
  • Permit students to replace written papers or essays with alternative projects, such as original films, dioramas, posters, or PowerPoint presentations.
  • Allow typing in place of handwriting.

Reading

  • Tests can be read aloud to students.
  • Consider using audiobooks with human narration, e-texts with text-to-speech software, or combo programs like LearningAlly audiobooks or Kindle Immersion reading to improve comprehension.
  • Allow students with impaired working memory and critical thinking skills to annotate print books using highlighters and sticky notes to help them comprehend and retain information.

Executive Function

  • Give students a second set of books to read at home.
  • Encourage them to complete seventy-five percent of the given homework.
  • During class, frequently ask the student how they plan to recall the rules or facts that were covered.

Emotion and Behavior

  • Offer a space for the student to take breaks and relax when needed. Make a plan in advance so the student can indicate to the teacher when it's time for a break and leave without drawing attention to themselves or causing a disturbance.
  • Arrange a weekly check-in with the special education instructor or guidance counselor.
  • If upset tantrums are a regular issue, have a behavior intervention plan (BIP) or crisis plan in place.

2. Instruction

The techniques you apply when teaching:

  • Provide simple, straightforward instructions. Use charts, props, and other visual tools.
  • Change up the tempo and incorporate a variety of activities. Energetic, high-intensity activities, such as competitive games, are beneficial for many ADHD students.
  • Establish an anonymous cue system with the student, like a little tap on the shoulder or a post-it note on their desk, as a gentle reminder to stay focused.
  • Offer regular breaks and provide a physical release, such as tapping something silent or squeezing a rubber ball.
  • Avoid asking a student with ADHD to carry out an assignment or provide a public response to a question that might be too challenging.

3. Intervention

The process of stopping actions that cause other students to get distracted or lose focus:

  • Work out some indicators with the student to prevent time-consuming behavior that distracts others. This could be indicated with a sticky note on the student's desk, a discreet shoulder squeeze, or a hand signal.
  • If you must talk about the student's behavior, have a private conversation. Additionally, if somewhat incorrect behavior is accidental and doesn't distract others or interfere with the lesson, attempt to overlook it.
